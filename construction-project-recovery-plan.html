Construction Project Recovery Plan

Turn cited cost, schedule, ESG, and leakage findings into practical 7/30/90 executive actions.

In one paragraph

In one paragraph: A construction project recovery plan converts evidence-backed findings into prioritized action. Constrovet uses cited cost leakage, overrun, schedule delay, ESG, procurement, and missing-evidence signals to create 7-day, 30-day, and 90-day actions for executive review. The plan is not a substitute for project governance, legal advice, or commercial negotiation. It helps teams decide what to verify, recover, correct, monitor, or escalate first, with each action tied back to available documents and assigned review ownership for follow-up.

Problem

Project reports often identify issues without assigning practical recovery steps. A delayed purchase order, material wastage, cost overrun, ESG gap, or schedule slippage may appear in a report, but executives still need a clear sequence of evidence checks, responsibilities, and review milestones.

How Constrovet helps

  • Turns cited findings into action-oriented recovery priorities.
  • Separates urgent evidence gaps from longer governance controls.
  • Links each action to cost, schedule, ESG, or procurement context.
  • Shows what can be reviewed immediately and what requires more evidence.
  • Creates executive-ready action summaries without inventing claims.

Data and documents required

  • Cost leakage and overrun findings with citations and calculations.
  • Schedule delay evidence, progress reports, and procurement records.
  • ESG, carbon, fuel, water, energy, waste, and emissions records where available.
  • Existing action trackers, project review notes, and stakeholder priorities.

FAQ

What is a construction project recovery plan?

It is a structured action plan that uses project evidence to prioritize urgent, 30-day, and 90-day recovery work across cost, schedule, ESG, and governance issues.

Does the plan make legal claims?

No. The plan organizes evidence-backed actions for review. Legal or commercial claims should be assessed by the appropriate advisors.

What inputs are needed?

Useful inputs include cited cost leakage findings, schedule delay evidence, procurement issues, ESG records, missing-evidence notes, and stakeholder priorities.

Why use 7/30/90 actions?

The 7/30/90 structure separates immediate evidence checks, near-term corrective work, and longer governance controls.

Who should use the recovery plan?

Project owners, contractors, project managers, developers, financiers, consultants, and executive teams can use it for review and coordination.
